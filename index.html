Velu Nachiyar: A Forgotten Queen of Resistance

Velu Nachiyar — commemorative image

Introduction

  • In the 18th century, Velu Nachiyar was one of the few women rulers who defied British rule in South India.
  • Her use of local customs, places of worship, and community activism for organized and strategic opposition was remarkable.

Personal Background

  • The year 1730 saw the birth of Velu Nachiyar, the daughter of Ramnad's royal family. Due to the absence of a male heir, she was raised like a princely child.
  • She was trained in military strategy, combat, and horse riding, and was fluent in languages such as Urdu, English, and some French.
  • After her marriage to Muthu Vaduganatha Thevar, she became the Queen of Sivaganga and actively participated in administration and military leadership.

Loss

  • In 1772, Sivaganga was attacked by British forces in alliance with the Nawab of Arcot.
  • Her husband was killed during a raid at the Kalaiyarkovil temple, which was also looted and destroyed.
  • Velu Nachiyar was forced to flee with her daughter, while Sivaganga was occupied and renamed Hussainnagar.

Vengeance

  • During eight years of exile, Velu Nachiyar built intelligence networks, allied with Haidar Ali, and trained a women’s army known as the Udaiyal Padai.
  • Her forces infiltrated Sivaganga by disguising themselves as devotees during the Vijayadashami festival.
  • Sivaganga was recaptured after her commander, Kuyili, sacrificed her life by destroying the British ammunition store.

Legacy

  • Velu Nachiyar became the first Indian queen to successfully reclaim her kingdom from the British.
  • She appointed the Marudhu brothers as chief minister and military commander while ruling on behalf of her daughter.
  • Her resistance influenced later anti-British movements, including the Poligar Wars in South India.

Why People Forgot About Her

  • Early anti-British uprisings by regional rulers were overshadowed by later national movements.
  • Colonial records minimized indigenous resistance, and women leaders received limited recognition.
  • Her story survived mainly through local worship, folk ballads, and oral traditions rather than mainstream history.
